    Fundação José Saramago - Casa dos Bicos

    43 Fundação José Saramago - Casa dos Bicos Castelo e Graça Equipamentos Até Séc. XVI 1950 – 2000 2000 – 2017 Francisco de Arruda, 1523 Manuel Vicente e José Daniel Santa-Rita, 1981 / João Santa-Rita, 2011 João Santa-Rita às 12h30 e por Sérgio Machado Letria às 14h30 e 16h30 Commissioned by Brás de Albuquerque, the son of the first Portuguese Viceroy of India, and built between 1521 and 1523, the Casa dos Bicos stands out for its unusual façade covered in “diamond-like spikes”, known by the popular term “bicos” [Portuguese for spikes or beaks]; the façade takes its inspiration from Italian Renaissance models. The earthquake of 1755 did considerable damage to the building. Later, after partial reconstruction, it housed a salt cod trader’s warehouse, which remained there well into the 20th century. In 1983, on the occasion of the 17th European Exposition of Arts, Science and Culture, a renovation project by Daniel Santa-Rita restored the building’s original volume. However, it remained unfinished, a testament to the memory of what had since disappeared. Subjected to further renovation work from 2008 to 2012, Casa dos Bicos is now the headquarters of the Fundação José Saramago and home to the Casa dos Bicos archaeological nucleus.
  • 50

    Igreja Sagrado Coração de Jesus

    50 Igreja Sagrado Coração de Jesus Av. Liberdade e Príncipe Real Equipamentos 1950 – 2000 Nuno Portas , Nuno Teotónio Pereira, Pedro Vieira de Almeida e outros, 1970 Cátia Santana Integrated in a larger parochial complex, this church dedicated to the Sacred Heart of Jesus, easily goes unnoticed by even the most attentive passer-by along the nearby street. Breaking with religious tradition, Nuno Portas and Nuno Teotónio Pereira returned to the original model of the Romanic church, emphasising the relationship between the sacred space and the surrounding community, in this case taken to the limit by the naked and urban character that the exposed concrete confers. Refined in its work with space, details and lighting, the church surprises the visitor with its monumental interior volumes. In 1975 it was awarded the Valmor Prize and it has been a National Monument since 2010.
  • 71

    Casa-Museu Dr. Anastácio Gonçalves

    71 Dr Anastácio Gonçalves Museum House Avenidas Novas e Benfica Equipamentos 1900 – 1950 Norte Júnior, 1904 Ana Mantua Built to a design by Norte Júnior in 1904/05, this house was commissioned by the painter José Malhoa to serve as his residence and studio. The first artist’s house in Lisbon, and winner of the Valmor Prize in 1905, in urban development terms it reflects the plans for expansion of the city and is an important example of late 19th-century Portuguese architecture. In 1932 the “Malhoa House” was purchased by Dr Anastácio Gonçalves, who lived and organised his collection there until his death in 1965. It was inaugurated as a Museum House in 1980 and underwent extension and renovation works in 1996, which included the annexation of a new house to the original building.
  • 81

    Museu Nacional do Azulejo - Convento da Madre de Deus

    81 Museu Nacional do Azulejo - Convento da Madre de Deus Oriente Palácios e Conventos Equipamentos Até Séc. XVI Séc. XVII – XVIII Séc. XIX vários autores Séc. XVI, XVIII, XIX e XX As visitas livres dão acesso apenas à exposição temporária e Convento da Madre de Deus Alexandre Pais The history of the azulejo tile in Portugal from the late 15th century to the present day is told in the former Madre Deus convent that was founded in 1509 by Queen Leonor. The convent was always received special attention from the Portuguese royal family and underwent several renovations over the centuries. Thanks to the interventions in the 17th and 18th centuries, the convent church, originally from 1550, is recognised as one of the foremost examples of the Baroque decorative style in Portugal.

    Teatro Nacional D. Maria II

    32 Teatro Nacional D. Maria II Bairro Alto e Baixa-Chiado Equipamentos Séc. XIX Fortunato Lodi, 1846 inclui acesso à exposição 'Teatro em cartaz: A coleção do D. Maria II, 1853-2015' Guias do Teatro Nacional D.Maria II; levantamento de bilhetes uma hora antes do início das visitas guiadas; limite de 2 bilhetes por pessoa This National Theatre was built to “contribute to the civilisation and moral betterment of the Portuguese nation”, in the words of Almeida Garrett who idealised this creative space and inaugurated it on 13 April 1846 to mark the 27th birthday of Queen Maria II (1819-1853). Built in a Neoclassical style, the building’s original design was by the Italian architect Fortunato Lodi. Following its construction, the theatre underwent a number of renovation interventions, including an interior reconstruction project under the guidance of Rebelo de Andrade after a serious fire in 1964. It did not reopen until 1978.

    Campo das Cebolas

    44 Campo das Cebolas Castelo e Graça Conjuntos Urbanos Espaços de Lazer 2000 – 2017 João Luís Carrilho da Graça, 2017 (reab.) Victor Beiramar Diniz The Portas do Mar plaza, which includes the current Campo das Cebolas and the space known as Ribeira Velha, next to the Praça do Comércio buildings, is seen as an opportunity to rethink the relationship between the city and the River Tagus. The intervention strategy includes creating a large open space – an opening up that makes it clear that the old city went as far as the line of the ancient walls, where Casa dos Bicos now stands – with a slight inclination upwards to Cathedral slope and the castle. At the other end, next to the waterfront avenue, is a flat area that establishes a direct relationship with the river. Underneath the new square is an underground parking facility with roughly 210 spaces, occupying the space between the old shorefront and the wall of the old Ver-o-Peso quays.
  • 59

    Cemitério dos Prazeres

    59 Prazeres Cemetery Campo de Ourique e Amoreiras Equipamentos Séc. XIX vários autores, 1833 Ema Câmara e Sérgio Palmeiro A great example of the Romantic style in Portugal, Prazeres Cemetery is the final resting place of many of the country’s most illustrious figures. A kind of open-air museum, it is a repository of important information on Portuguese modern history, architecture, urban planning, sculpture, as well as the changes approaches to the idea of death over the years. With its ambience marked by the serenity of the impressive cypresses, to walk through this space is to learn the history documented on its countless tombs. This tour will visit a number of tombs, in particular the interior of the Palmela Tomb, the largest private tomb in Europe.
  • 75

    Centro Ismaili

    75 Centro Ismaili Avenidas Novas e Benfica Equipamentos 1950 – 2000 Raj Rewal Associates e Frederico Valsassina Arquitectos, 1998 Guias do Centro Ismaili Lisbon’s Ismaili Centre was the first facility in Continental Europe that combined meeting spaces for Ismaili Muslims with the installations of an international social, cultural and economic development network for people of all faiths, backgrounds and origins. Built around a number of patios, the buildings integrate multi-use spaces and open areas designed for social functions and cultural and educational events. The design, a rare example of Islam-inspired architecture in Portugal, nods to the geometric patterns in traditional Muslim arts while allying contemporary building techniques and local materials.

    Museu de Lisboa - Teatro Romano

    42 Museu de Lisboa - Teatro Romano Castelo e Graça Equipamentos Até Séc. XVI Séc. XVII – XVIII Séc. XIX 2000 – 2017 Daniela Ermano e João Carrasco, 2015 (reab.) Lídia Fernandes às 11h00 e por João Carrasco às 17h A visit to this space located on the slope below the castle becomes a journey back in time to the early 1st century AD. The museum’s activities centre on the discovery and interpretation of the remains of the Roman Theatre, which was abandoned in the 4th century AD and rediscovered during the reconstruction work after the 1755 earthquake. Fast forward to the 21st century and the creation of a small museological nucleus that has since grown and is now part of the Museum of Lisbon and its wider scope. Along the visitor course, which begins with a surprising tour of the city, the journey through time reveals the Roman ruins and other spaces taken under the museum’s wing, such as a 17th-century building and a large industrial space from the late 19th century.
